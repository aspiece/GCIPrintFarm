Simple vs. Complex — Which Prints Better?

✅ Simple Logo — Great for Printing
  • 🟢 Bold, thick shapes with clear outlines
  • 🟢 Minimal fine lines or detail
  • 🟢 Strong, recognizable silhouette
  • 🟢 No tiny text or microscopic details
  • 🟢 Converts cleanly to SVG
  • 🟢 Easy to edit in TinkerCAD
❌ Complex Logo — Printing Challenges
  • 🔴 Many thin lines and micro-details
  • 🔴 Small text within the logo
  • 🔴 Overlapping shapes and gradients
  • 🔴 Multiple colors (we print in one color)
  • 🔴 SVG conversion creates messy paths
  • 🔴 Hard to scale without losing detail
6 / 21
  • Use real examples if you have them. Pull up 2 logos on your browser — one simple school logo and one complex crest. Ask "Which would you choose?"
  • Key teaching moment: "3D printing is a single color, single material process for us. A logo that depends on color to be readable (like the colors of a flag) won't translate to a 3D print — the shape is all you have."
  • Good rule of thumb to share: "Imagine your logo as a shadow — just a flat black silhouette. If you can still tell what it is, it will probably print well. If it's unrecognizable as a silhouette, look for a simpler version."
  • Student action: After this slide, give students 60 seconds to look at their logo and decide if it's simple or complex. Discuss as needed.
⏱ 3 min

Why Simple Designs Print Better

  • Printer nozzle is 0.4 mm wide — it cannot reproduce lines thinner than that
  • At keychain scale (50 mm), tiny details disappear or merge together
  • Complex SVGs have many paths — TinkerCAD can struggle to import them cleanly
  • Simple outlines = stronger walls and cleaner prints
🎯

A logo that prints clearly at 50 mm is more impressive than a complex logo that comes out as a blob.

Clean beats complicated.

7 / 21
  • The 0.4 mm nozzle fact is powerful — students immediately understand why hair-thin lines won't print.
  • Relate to scale: A 1 cm wide element on a screen becomes 5 mm when scaled for a keychain. If it was already thin on screen, at 5 mm it disappears entirely.
  • Encourage simplification: "If your logo has a complicated crest, see if there's a simpler version — often there's a logo-mark or icon version that's just the mascot head or a letter. That's the one to use."
⏱ 3 min

Choosing a Printable Logo

💡 5 Rules for a Logo That Will Print Successfully:

  • Bold shapes win — thick outlines and filled areas print clearly at keychain size (40–60 mm)
  • Avoid tiny text — letters smaller than ~5 mm will blur or disappear in the print
  • Fewer shapes = easier SVG — the more separate pieces, the harder it is to clean up the SVG conversion
  • Check the silhouette — could you recognize your logo if it was a solid black shape? If yes, it'll print well
  • When in doubt, simplify — a clean simple design always beats a messy complex one at keychain scale

  • Transition: "OK — let's get your logo and start the conversion. Open your Downloads folder and find your logo, then go to express.adobe.com."

🎓 Step 2

Convert to SVG — Adobe Express

How to convert

  • Go to express.adobe.com
  • Find: Convert to SVG tool (search or in tools)
  • Upload your PNG/JPG logo
  • Review the converted SVG — check for clean edges
  • Download as .svg
  • Rename: YourName_Logo.svg

What a good SVG looks like

  • Clean, smooth edges — no jagged pixels
  • Recognizable as your original logo
  • No excess white or gray boxes around the shape
  • Preview it in a browser tab — should look like a clean vector
10 / 21
  • Demo on projector first — upload a sample logo and show the conversion live. Let students see what a clean SVG looks like before they do it themselves.
  • Common issues: (1) White background becomes a white filled rectangle in SVG — students need to find a transparent-background PNG. (2) Very complex logos produce messy SVGs — redirect to a simpler version. (3) Adobe Express requires a free account — make sure students have one or use a class account.
  • Alternative tool if Adobe Express isn't available: convertio.co or svgomg.net for SVG cleanup. Have a backup plan ready.
  • Quality check: Walk around and look at each student's SVG preview. If it looks like a tangle of lines, they need a simpler logo. Intervene early — don't let students carry a bad SVG into TinkerCAD.
  • Transition: "Brain break next — when you come back, we're importing this SVG into TinkerCAD."

🎓 Step 3

Import SVG into TinkerCAD

How to import

  • In TinkerCAD → open your keychain design
  • Click Import button (top toolbar)
  • Select your .svg file
  • Set scale at import — start with 25–50% or type a size
  • Click Import — the SVG appears as a 3D shape

After importing

  • The SVG imports with a default height — adjust it (2–3 mm looks good)
  • Position it on top of your keychain base
  • Use the height arrows to lift it slightly above the base if needed
  • Check that the logo shape looks right — resize if needed
13 / 21
  • Live demo immediately: Show the import on the projector with your staged demo. Import, scale, position, adjust height. Do it slowly so students can follow along.
  • Height setting: The SVG imports as a 3D extrusion. For a raised-logo effect, set height to 2–3 mm. For an engraved effect, set the SVG as a Hole and group it with the base — advanced option for students who want a challenge.
  • Common import issue: SVG comes in extremely large (hundreds of mm). Scale it down at the import dialog — type "30" in the scale width field to force it to 30 mm wide.
  • Second common issue: SVG imports as many separate pieces (each letter or shape separate). Help students group everything together (Ctrl+A to select all, then Ctrl+G to group) before positioning.
⏱ 8 min
🎯 Step 4 — Student Work

Build the Final Keychain

In TinkerCAD — combine everything:

  • Position your logo SVG on the keychain base
  • Confirm the keyring hole is still correct (5 mm diameter, grouped)
  • Scale the full design to 40–60 mm in the longest dimension
  • Make sure no part is thinner than 2 mm
  • Group all components: base + logo + hole = one object
  • Save and rename: Name_Keychain_Final
✋ Raise your hand before exporting — get a quick teacher check on your design.
14 / 21
  • Circulate constantly during this 8 minutes. Common issues: (1) Logo imported too large or too small. (2) Logo floating in mid-air — needs to be placed on the base (move down on Z axis). (3) Keyring hole was ungrouped by mistake — check it still works. (4) Base and logo not aligned — use the Align tool.
  • Quick approval: Before each student exports, give their TinkerCAD design a 30-second visual check. Look for: correct size, hole present and grouped, logo positioned correctly, nothing floating in air.
  • For advanced students: Suggest adding their name, a pattern to the back, or beveling the edges.
  • After 8 min: Pause the class and have 2 students share their designs on the projector. This normalizes diverse approaches and keeps motivation high.

📚 Vocabulary

Key Terms — SVG & Design

SVG (Scalable Vector Graphics) Image format stored as math (lines/curves) not pixels. Can be scaled without losing quality. TinkerCAD reads SVG files.
Vector vs. Raster Vector = math-based (SVG). Raster = pixel-based (PNG/JPG). TinkerCAD needs vector. Adobe Express converts raster → vector.
Silhouette The outer shape of a design when viewed as a solid. A strong silhouette = a design that prints clearly at small scale.
Logo Complexity How many elements, thin lines, and details a logo has. Simple logos (bold, thick) print better than complex ones at keychain scale.
15 / 21
  • Quick vocabulary pause during work time — review while students continue working on their designs.
  • Ask: "Why can't you just drag a PNG into TinkerCAD?" (TinkerCAD doesn't understand pixels — it needs vectors/paths to create a 3D shape.)

⏱ 5 min
🎓 Step 5

Export + Slice in Bambu Studio

Export from TinkerCAD

  • Click Export → .STL
  • Filename: Name_Keychain_Final.stl
  • Save to your class folder

In Bambu Studio

  • Import the STL
  • Settings: 0.2 mm layer height · 20% infill · supports off
  • Click Slice → review Preview
  • Check print time (< 2 hours is fine)

Save the 3MF file

  • After slicing → File → Save Project
  • Filename: Name_Keychain_Final.3mf
  • This is the file you'll send to the printer on Day 5
  • Keep both STL (source) and 3MF (print-ready)
16 / 21
  • This is the most critical file management moment of the entire lab. A lost 3MF on print day = student can't print. Be explicit about where files should be saved.
  • Naming convention is not optional: "If your file is named 'keychain.3mf' and so is your neighbor's, we have a problem on print day. Your name must be in the filename."
  • What to check in Preview: (1) Logo appears raised/engraved as expected. (2) Keyring hole goes all the way through (visible in layer preview). (3) First layer is solid. (4) Print time is reasonable.
  • If a student's logo disappears in Bambu Studio: It was either too thin (below 0.2 mm) in TinkerCAD, or the SVG import was a hole instead of a solid. Go back to TinkerCAD and increase the logo extrusion height.

✅ Print Readiness Checklist

Is Your Design Ready to Print?

  • STL exported and saved — named with your name
  • 3MF saved in Bambu Studio — named with your name
  • Preview checked — keyring hole visible going through the design
  • Preview checked — logo is visible and readable at keychain size
  • Print time is under 2 hours
  • No obvious gaps, missing walls, or floating shapes in the Preview
  • Files saved in your class folder — not just in Downloads
17 / 21
  • Walk through this list with students as a whole class, then give 2 minutes for students to verify each item on their own machine.
  • Teacher sign-off: Consider having each student show you the Bambu Studio Preview before you sign off on their design. A 30-second check per student during this time is realistic.
  • Students who aren't print-ready: Identify them now — they'll need targeted support. Common reasons: logo too small in the preview, hole not cutting through, or file naming issues. Address them before exit ticket.

✅ Check for Understanding

Quick Check

  1. Why can't you import a PNG directly into TinkerCAD?
  2. What does SVG stand for, and why does it scale without losing quality?
  3. What should you check in the Bambu Studio Preview before approving a print?
  4. Why does a simple logo print better than a complex one at keychain size?
  5. What two files should you have saved by end of class today?
19 / 21
  • Answers: (1) TinkerCAD needs vector (path) data, not pixel data. (2) Scalable Vector Graphics — stored as math, not pixels. (3) Keyring hole, logo visibility, layer count, print time, first layer. (4) Nozzle is 0.4 mm; tiny details get lost at keychain scale; fewer paths = cleaner import. (5) STL (source) and 3MF (print-ready).
