⏱ 5 min

What is 3D Printing?

  • Additive manufacturing — material is added, not cut away
  • Builds objects layer by layer from the bottom up
  • Uses a digital 3D model as the blueprint
  • Most common type for beginners: FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling)
  • The printer melts plastic and deposits it in precise paths
🏗️

Think of building a skyscraper one floor at a time — except the "floors" are 0.2 mm thin layers of plastic.

5 / 22
  • Key analogy to use: "Think of stacking very thin pancakes. Each pancake is one layer. Thousands of pancakes stacked together create a solid object."
  • Contrast with subtractive manufacturing: CNC machines remove material (like carving). 3D printers ADD material. Ask: "Why might adding material be better than removing it for some things?" (Less waste, can make complex hollow shapes.)
  • FDM stands for Fused Deposition Modeling — "fused" = melted together. You don't need students to memorize this but it's useful if it comes up.
  • Watch for: Students conflating 3D printing with laser cutting (also common in makerspaces) — laser cutting is subtractive and 2D.
  • Transition: "So how does FDM actually work step by step? Let's zoom in."

6 / 22
  • Walk through each step slowly. Ask students to visualize a Bambu printer's print head moving back and forth as you describe each step.
  • Temperature context: 200°C is about twice the temperature of boiling water. PLA melts around 190–210°C; PETG at 220–250°C. That's why you must never touch the nozzle during or after a print.
  • Ask check question: "If a layer is 0.2 mm thick and the final object is 20 mm tall, how many layers is that?" (Answer: 100 layers.) This helps students appreciate the scale.
  • Common student question: "Why does the first layer matter so much?" — Because all subsequent layers rest on it. A bad first layer = a failed print. This is why bed leveling is critical (coming up in the demo).
  • Transition: "Now let's zoom out and see the full pipeline from idea to finished object."

📚 Vocabulary

Key Terms — Printer Parts

Extruder Grips and pushes filament into the hot nozzle. If it slips or jams, the filament stops feeding.
Nozzle The metal tip that melts filament and deposits it. Temperature matters — too cold = clog, too hot = stringing.
Build Plate / Print Bed The flat surface where your print is built. Must be clean and level for the first layer to stick.
Bed Leveling Ensuring the nozzle is the right distance from the plate across its whole surface. Bambu printers do this automatically.
9 / 22
  • Read each term and definition aloud. Have students repeat the term after you.
  • Quick check: Point to a picture or a real printer part and ask "What am I pointing at?" before moving on.
  • Reinforce: "You'll see all four of these terms on the matching activity in the second half of class — make sure they stick."
  • Watch for: Students confusing the extruder and the nozzle. The extruder is the mechanism that PUSHES filament. The nozzle is where filament comes OUT.
⏱ 3 min

Key Printer Parts

  • Nozzle — Melts & places filament (most common failure point)
  • Extruder — Pushes filament toward the nozzle
  • Build plate — Print surface; must be clean for adhesion
  • Filament spool — Holds the plastic; must feed smoothly
  • X/Y/Z axes — Control movement in three directions
🖨️

During the demo you'll see all of these parts in action on a real Bambu printer.

🎓 Teacher Demo

The Extruder

What it does

  • Grips the filament with a drive gear
  • Pushes filament into the heated nozzle at a controlled rate
  • Controls how much material is deposited per movement

What to show students

  • Point to the extruder housing on the printer
  • Show where filament enters and exits
  • Demonstrate what a clog looks like (extruder moves, nothing comes out)
  • Show proper vs. improper filament loading angle
14 / 22
  • Physically point to the extruder on the real printer — say its name aloud while touching it.
  • Key analogy: "The extruder is like a very precise toothpaste tube squeezer — it controls exactly how much plastic comes out and when."
  • Most common student question: "What happens when the extruder jams?" Answer: the print head keeps moving but no filament comes out — you get air gaps or a completely empty print. This is called "under-extrusion."
  • Safety point: The extruder mechanism can pinch fingers if it's moving. Do not put fingers in the drive gear area during a print.
  • Transition: "The extruder gets the filament to the nozzle. But where does the nozzle put it? On the build plate — and that plate needs to be perfectly set up. That's bed leveling."
⏱ 5 min
🎓 Teacher Demo

Bed Leveling

Why it matters

  • Nozzle must be the same distance from the plate everywhere
  • Too close → nozzle drags through plastic, clogs
  • Too far → filament falls instead of sticking
  • A bad first layer = a failed print

On a Bambu printer

  • Automatic bed leveling happens before every print
  • The printer probes multiple points on the plate
  • Creates a mesh map of the surface
  • Adjusts Z height dynamically during the first layer
15 / 22
  • Show the leveling process by starting a print (or showing a video clip of auto-leveling) — students can visually see the probe touching multiple points.
  • Key analogy: "Imagine trying to write on paper that's on a bumpy table. The pen drags in some spots and barely touches in others. Bed leveling makes sure the 'table' (build plate) is as flat as possible relative to the 'pen' (nozzle)."
  • Common student question: "Does the Bambu always level by itself?" Yes — but you still need to make sure the plate is seated correctly before the print starts. Auto-leveling compensates for minor differences; it can't fix a badly misaligned plate.
  • Watch for: Students thinking auto-leveling means you never have to think about the bed. Emphasize that a clean, correctly placed plate is a prerequisite for auto-leveling to work well.
⏱ 3 min
🎓 Teacher Demo

Bed Plate Alignment

✅ Correct placement

  • Plate sits flat and is fully seated on the magnetic base
  • No gaps between plate and frame
  • Plate does not rock or shift when touched
  • Handle plate by the edges only — fingerprints = poor adhesion

❌ Common mistakes

  • Plate is tilted or partially lifted at one edge
  • Plate has residue or oils from fingers
  • Plate removed and reinserted upside-down
  • Wrong type of plate used for the filament
16 / 22
  • Physically demonstrate: Remove the plate, show correct grip (edges only), place it back correctly, then show what a misaligned plate looks like.
  • Safety/handling rule to state explicitly: "Touching the print surface with your fingers leaves oils that prevent the print from sticking. Always handle by the edges. If the plate gets dirty, spray it with dish soap and water and wipe clean."
  • Bambu-specific note: Bambu printers use magnetic build plates. The plate should snap into place smoothly — if you have to force it, something is wrong.
  • Transition: "Now that you've seen the three critical things — extruder, bed leveling, and plate alignment — let's see if you can identify the parts on your own. Open your laptops — we're doing the matching activity."

📚 Vocabulary

Key Terms — Filament

Filament The plastic wire that a 3D printer melts and deposits. Comes on spools, usually 1.75 mm diameter.
PLA Most common beginner filament. Made from plant-based materials. Easy to print; lower heat resistance.
PETG Tougher, more heat-resistant than PLA. Harder to print with but better for parts that need durability.
18 / 22
  • Quick, high-energy vocab review — you're building to the comparison table on the next slide.
  • Hold up spools of PLA and PETG if you have them — physical props dramatically help students remember which is which.
  • Say: "For most of what we do in this class, you'll be using PLA. PETG comes into play when someone needs something stronger — like a part that gets hot or handles stress."
⏱ 4 min

PLA vs PETG

✅ PLA — Use for most projects
  • 🟢 Easy to print — fewer settings to worry about
  • 🟢 Low warping — great first layer adhesion
  • 🟢 Many colors available
  • 🟡 Lower heat resistance (softens ~60°C)
  • 🟡 Less flexible — can be brittle
🔵 PETG — Use when you need toughness
  • 🟢 Stronger and more flexible than PLA
  • 🟢 Higher heat resistance (~80°C)
  • 🟢 Good for functional parts
  • 🔴 More challenging to print — stringier
  • 🔴 Needs higher temperatures & slower speeds
19 / 22
  • Practical guidance: "For your keychains in this class — use PLA. It prints cleanly, comes in great colors, and is easy to manage. We'd only switch to PETG if you needed the keychain to survive being left in a hot car."
  • Common student question: "Can you mix PLA and PETG?" Technically yes with a multi-material printer, but it requires careful settings. Not relevant for this class.
  • Common student question: "Which is more expensive?" PETG is generally slightly more expensive, but both are affordable — typically $20–30 per kg.
  • After this slide: Transition to the MakerWorld exploration. "Now let's get hands-on — open MakerWorld and find something you'd actually want to print."
