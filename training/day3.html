🗂️ Common 3D Printing File Types

Before you design, you need to know what kinds of files are used in the 3D printing process. Here are the three you will encounter most often.

📐

STL

The Shape

STL (Standard Tessellation Language) is one of the most common 3D printing file types. It stores the shape of an object as a mesh of triangles. STL files do not include color, materials, or print settings — just the geometry.

✨

3MF

Smarter Design File

3MF (3D Manufacturing Format) is a newer, smarter format. In addition to shape, it can store color, materials, scale, orientation, and print settings. Bambu Studio uses 3MF files to save your full project.

🤖

G-code

Printer Instructions

G-code is the machine language created by your slicer (Bambu Studio). It tells the printer exactly where to move, how fast to go, and when to heat up. G-code is what actually runs the printer.

💡 Big Idea:  STL = the shape  |  3MF = a smarter design file  |  G-code = the printer instructions

📚 Vocabulary

STL — Stores only the shape of a 3D model as a mesh of triangles.
3MF — A newer file format that stores shape plus color, materials, and settings.
G-code — Machine instructions created by the slicer that tell the printer exactly what to do.
Slicer — Software (like Bambu Studio) that converts a 3D model into G-code layers.
Workplane — The flat surface in Tinkercad where you build your 3D designs.

Step 4 of 7 Locked Design Your Name Tag
📌 Instructions: Follow the steps below to create a name tag or keychain design in Tinkercad. Work through each step in order. Raise your hand if you get stuck.
🧠 Why this design? A simple flat shape with text is perfect for learning Tinkercad basics. The same skills you practice today are what you will need for your school logo keychain later in the unit.

📋 Design Requirements

  • Create a base shape (box or rounded box)
  • Add text or a simple design feature
  • Include a small cylinder hole for a keyring
  • Keep the overall size roughly 60 × 30 × 5 mm or smaller
  • Keep the design clean and printable

🔢 Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Open Tinkercad — go to tinkercad.com and sign in with your school account. Click Create new design.
  2. Add your base shape — from the Basic Shapes panel on the right, drag a Box or Rounded Box onto the workplane. This is the main body of your name tag.
  3. Resize the base — click the shape to select it. Use the dimension boxes at the bottom to set the size to approximately 60 mm wide × 30 mm deep × 5 mm tall.
  4. Add a Text shape — from the Basic Shapes panel, drag a Text shape onto the workplane. Click it and type your name or a short word. Resize the text to fit on your base, then move it so it sits on top of the base shape.
  5. Add a cylinder for the keyring hole — drag a Cylinder shape onto the workplane. In the shape inspector (right panel), set it as a Hole. Resize it to about 5 mm wide and slightly taller than your base. Position it near one short end of the base.
  6. Align your shapes — select all shapes using Ctrl + A. Click Align in the toolbar and center everything as needed so it looks clean.
  7. Group everything — select all shapes using Ctrl + A, then click Group (or press Ctrl + G). This merges your base, text, and hole into one solid object.
  8. Name your design — click the design title at the top and give it a clear name like Day3_NameTag_YourName. Tinkercad saves automatically.

Step 5 of 7 Locked Export & Bambu Studio Preview
📌 Instructions: Export your Tinkercad design as an STL file and import it into Bambu Studio to see how your design would be prepared for printing.
🔁 This is the handoff moment. Your design moves from the design tool to the slicer — like handing a blueprint to the factory. You are not printing today, just previewing.

📤 Export from Tinkercad

  1. In Tinkercad, click the Export button in the top-right corner.
  2. Choose STL format. Tinkercad will download the file to your computer.
  3. Rename the file clearly: Day3_NameTag_YourName.stl

🖥️ Import into Bambu Studio

  1. Open Bambu Studio on your computer.
  2. Drag your STL file onto the print bed, or use File → Import.
  3. Check that your model appears correctly on the print bed — it should look like your design in Tinkercad.
  4. Look at the size indicator to confirm your design is small and would print reasonably quickly. No need to slice today — just preview!
